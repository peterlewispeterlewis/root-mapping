# Root Mapping — Quick Reference

A Root Map is a shared, evolving record of the outcomes the team believes a product must produce to meet customers' needs and yield sustainable business advantages, visualized in a hierarchy of enabling relationships.

## Levels of Zoom

| Level | Scope | Description |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| **Platform / Organization** | Largest | Broader outcomes to which the product contributes |
| **Product** | Root | Overall value to deliver — the differentiated value proposition |
| **Feature Categories** | Branches | Groupings of core functionality to address anchor needs |
| **Features** | Leaves | Core functionality focused on discrete needs |
| **Sub-features** | Detail | Specific capabilities necessary to deliver core feature value |

Move **down** a level by asking: *"What smaller problems need to be solved in order to sufficiently meet this need or produce this outcome?"*

Move **up** a level by asking: *"What broader need does this point to for customers (so that they can ____)? What broader initiative or strategy does this business priority enable?"*

## Ingredients of a Root Map

- **Customer outcomes** — what's now true for customers if the product succeeds
- **Inferred needs** — the customer problems each outcome addresses
- **Business outcomes** — sustainable advantages the business gains (retention, revenue, market expansion — NOT features or solutions)
- **Solutions** — the latest proposal (product or feature) to produce the outcomes
- **Hierarchy of enabling relationships** — arrows connecting child outcomes upward to parent outcomes
- **Essential Value Scope (EVS)** — the irreducible core; without these outcomes, customers won't want it

## Navigation: Starting from Different Places

### Starting with a customer outcome
Ask: *"Why do we believe creating this customer outcome will produce a unique business advantage? What solution might be most effective in enabling these customer and business outcomes?"*

### Starting with a business outcome
Ask: *"With which customer needs does this business priority most naturally overlap? Why does the business believe this is a resonant problem area to tackle?"*

### Starting with a solution
Ask: *"What is the customer problem to which this might be the answer? If effective, what business outcomes might it produce?"*

### The core questions (regardless of starting point)
- **Customer outcome:** Regardless of how we enable it, what should our customers now be able to do because of our product?
- **Need:** What need is insufficiently addressed by the market?
- **Solution:** What solution, if effective, might produce these business and customer outcomes?
- **Business outcome:** What sustainable competitive advantage is vital to the business now and in the future?

## Sample Root Map: Waze

Peter Lewis's original article uses Waze as the primary example — a simple, clear map with two main branches:

1. **"Fight traffic"** — the core utility (navigation, real-time routing, hazard avoidance)
2. **"Do it together"** — the community layer (user reports, social features, gamification)

Both branches enable the root outcome: *effortless, stress-free driving*. The Waze example demonstrates that even a complex product can have a simple, clear map when outcomes are well-articulated.
